Absorption spectra

What is it?

Spectrum presenting a sequence of frequencies, in a relatively wide interval, corresponding to the photons absorbed by a substance, generally representing the absorption coefficient as a function frequency or wavelength.

=⇒ At the molecular level, the system undergoes a transition from a state of (lower) energy to one of (higher) energy Em, thanks to the energy of the photon (hv):

$$A(En) + hν → A⋆(Em).$$

What does it mean?

The absorption spectrum of a material shows the fraction of the incident electromagnetic radiation that a material absorbs within a range of frequencies. It is, in a sense, the opposite of an emission spectrum.

What does it have to do with the electromagnetic spectrum?

The absorption spectrum is related to the electromagnetic spectrum as it is a graphical representation of the frequencies of radiation electromagnetic that are absorbed by a substance. In other words, an absorption spectrum shows the frequencies or wavelengths of light that are absorbed by a given material. These absorption frequencies are related to the transitions between different energy states of molecules or atoms that make up the material. Therefore, the absorption spectrum is an important tool used in spectroscopy, which is the study of the study of the interaction of light with matter.
On the other hand, the electromagnetic spectrum, represents the entire range of frequencies or wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation, from low-frequency radio waves to gamma rays. Both concepts are closely related in the physical explanation of the phenomena associated with the interaction of light with matter.

What does it have to do with chemistry?

The absorption spectrum is a very useful analytical tool in chemistry. With it, chemists can determine properties and structures of molecules, atoms and compounds. By observing the absorption spectrum of a sample of material, chemists can identify which wavelengths of light are absorbed and by how much, allowing them to deduce information about the chemical structure of the sample. Therefore, absorption spectra are an important tool in analytical chemistry and in the investigation of structure and properties of molecules and chemical compounds.

How do you get the absorption spectra of a substance?

The simplest approach to absorption spectroscopy is to generate radiation with a source, measure a reference spectrum of that radiation with a detector, and then re-measure the spectrum of the sample after placing the material of interest between the source and detector.

Determine the composition of the stars by means of the absorption spectrum.

Stars

Absorption lines in stellar spectra can be used to determine the chemical composition of a star. Each element is responsible for a different set of absorption lines in the spectrum, at wavelengths that can be measured extremely reliably by laboratory experiments. Therefore, an absorption line at a particular wavelength in a stellar spectrum shows that element must be present. The absorption lines of hydrogen (found in the atmosphere of almost any star) are particularly important. The lines of hydrogen found within the visible spectrum are called Balmer lines.

Sun

The absorption spectrum of the sun contains many lines corresponding to atomic elements and small molecules in a state outside the local thermodynamic equilibrium for which the corresponding calculations haven´t yet been obtained using quantum mechanics.

Planets and asteroids

Planets and asteroids shine only by reflecting light from the Sun. The reflected light contains absorption bands due to minerals present in the rocks of rocky bodies, or elements and molecules present in the atmospheres of gas giants. Asteroids can be classified into three main types, according to their spectrum: C-type asteroids are composed of carbonaceous materials; S-type asteroids consist mainly of silicates; and M-type asteroids are 'metallic'. C- and S-type asteroids are the most common.

Comets

The spectrum of comets consists of a solar spectrum reflected in the surrounding dust clouds, as well as emission lines formed when the solar wind collides with the gases surrounding the comet. Compositional analysis of comets has shown that they are made of virgin materials from the formative times of the solar system. Many organic compounds are known to exist in comets, and it has been suggested that cometary impacts may have provided Earth with much of the water in its oceans and the compounds necessary for the formation of life. It has been suggested that life may have been brought to Earth by comets from interstellar space (the Panspermia theory).
